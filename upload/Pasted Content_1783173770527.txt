\section{Introduction}

Digital platforms have altered the conditions under which Muslim learners encounter, trust, contest, and perform religious knowledge. In contemporary Islamic Religious Education (IRE), scholars, teachers, and institutions now share the field with influencers, peer networks, search engines, recommender systems, and generative AI\@. The result is a fragmented authority ecology in which visibility can be mistaken for credibility (Samudra et al., 2024, Muhamad Arif et al., 2025, Saidah et al., 2024). The pedagogical problem is therefore epistemic as much as technical. Learners must be taught how religious claims are verified, contextualised, and ethically acted upon.

The article uses the term post-truth critically rather than as a purely Western diagnostic label. Post-truth literature identifies weakened public standards of justification, affective circulation, and distrust of expertise (Fuller, 2018, Lewandowsky et al., 2017, McIntyre, 2018). In IRE, however, the problem must also be read through Islamic epistemic categories such as \textit{haqq}, \textit{haqiqa}, \textit{ilm}, \textit{yaqin}, \textit{zann}, and \textit{tabayyun}. The educational issue is not only whether learners can identify misinformation, but whether they can distinguish reliable religious knowledge from persuasive visibility while remaining accountable to scholarship, adab, and public benefit (Nasr, 1996, Ramadan, 2009, Zaman, 2004).

Recent studies offer several useful entry points, including digital media in the classroom, teacher competence, online authority, student motivation, Islamic digital literacy, and trust in platform-mediated sources (Adima et al., 2024, Erihadiana et al., 2025, Istiani and Islamy, 2025, Miftachul Huda et al., 2024). What remains less visible is how these issues converge in ordinary classroom practice. In one online lesson, learners may need to ask who is speaking, why a source deserves trust, how a message shapes identity, and what ethical response is expected. This review therefore treats digital IRE as disciplined religious discernment that helps learners distinguish scholarship from persuasive visibility, use online content without losing Islamic epistemic grounding, and participate in digital spaces responsibly.

Three limitations are especially consequential. Student perspectives on authority and authenticity remain underdeveloped, comparative work beyond Indonesia and Malaysia is still limited despite the transnational circulation of digital religious content (Berglund and Gent, 2018, Sahin, 2018, Selod, 2018), and longitudinal evidence remains scarce. The review therefore speaks most directly to digital IRE debates shaped by Southeast Asian evidence, while using wider Islamic education and religious education literature to interpret transferable issues. These gaps leave the long-term formation of religious judgement insufficiently understood. This synthesis addresses them by integrating authority, authenticity, pedagogy, identity, and ethics within one systematic review design.

\subsection{Review Gap and Research Questions}

This review is positioned at the intersection of digital mediation, religious authority, and pedagogical formation. Rather than treating technology integration as an isolated instructional concern, it asks how IRE can sustain credible religious knowing when learners move between classrooms, families, scholarly traditions, peer networks, influencers, search engines, and algorithmic feeds. Earlier reviews have clarified important developments in teacher competence, digital media use, and literacy-oriented IRE, but they rarely synthesise these strands around the combined problems of authority and authenticity (Adima et al., 2024, Istiani and Islamy, 2025, Samudra et al., 2024). The gap is therefore not the absence of work on digital IRE, but the lack of an integrated account of how pedagogical practice helps Muslim learners recognise qualified authority, understand religious claims responsibly, and form identity within contested digital spaces.

Guided by this gap, the review asks how IRE responds to fragmented religious authority in digital contexts, what pedagogies cultivate authentic religious understanding, how learners negotiate Muslim identity across school, family, scholarly, peer, and platform-mediated spaces, and what ethical and institutional implications follow for responsible digital IRE\@. These questions orient the synthesis toward practical judgement rather than catalogue description. Its purpose is to connect evidence on pedagogy, authority, authenticity, identity, and ethics in order to clarify how digital IRE can support disciplined religious discernment in the post-truth era.

\section{Methodology}

This systematic integrative literature review is grounded in a pragmatic research paradigm that can accommodate qualitative, quantitative, mixed-methods, and review-level evidence (Creswell and Plano Clark, 2017, Creswell and Poth, 2018, Dixon-Woods et al., 2005). Pragmatism is appropriate for reviewing complex educational phenomena because it emphasises the practical consequences of evidence and its relevance to educational practice (Johnson and Onwuegbuzie, 2004). Within this orientation, the analysis was inductive. Themes and patterns were allowed to emerge from the corpus rather than being tested against predetermined hypotheses (Creswell and Poth, 2018, Thomas, 2006).

An integrative systematic design was appropriate for three reasons. First, it provides a transparent procedure for locating, screening, and synthesising existing research (Gough et al., 2017, Petticrew and Roberts, 2006). Second, it supports the identification of patterns, gaps, and contradictions across qualitative, quantitative, and review-level material on IRE pedagogy in post-truth digital conditions (Dixon-Woods et al., 2005, Sandelowski and Barroso, 2007). Third, it is well suited to emerging phenomena that cross disciplinary boundaries, including religious education, Islamic studies, educational technology, and media studies.

Searches drew on four academic sources, namely Scopus, Web of Science, ERIC, and Google Scholar. These sources were selected because they index or expose peer-reviewed journals across education, religious studies, Islamic studies, educational technology, and media studies. Search terms were organised into three thematic groups covering Islamic Religious Education, digital technology, and post-truth phenomena. The complete search string was as follows.

Searching took place between January and March 2026 and was restricted to publications from 2015--2025. This range captures the growth of digital technologies in educational settings and the development of post-truth phenomena while keeping the evidence base current. Language was limited to English and Indonesian; Arabic and other Islamic scholarly languages were therefore treated as a transferability boundary rather than as evidence searched within the corpus. Google Scholar served as a supplementary discovery source rather than as an equivalent indexed database. Its contribution was bounded to the first 701 relevance-ranked hits returned during the search window; screening stopped when 100 consecutive results yielded no additional stable, peer-reviewed, IRE-relevant full text. Eligible Google Scholar records were manually checked for venue, full-text stability, and duplication against the indexed-database pool. This bounded approach was intended to improve reliability and transparency in line with lessons from systematic review practice (Haddaway et al., 2015). Table~\ref{tab.search-coverage} reports the source-level coverage.

After the main corpus had been completed, a supplementary Arabic-context scoping check was inspected to stress-test the review's language and student-voice limitations. The check used three broader English Boolean strings focused on digital or online Islamic education and authority, student voice or agency and religious identity, and Arabic or Arab contexts combined with digital education and identity. It reported broad returns across Scopus, Web of Science, PubMed and Google Scholar, but it was not merged into the PRISMA corpus because it was post hoc, broader than the reported operational search logic, and mixed Arabic-context English-language records with many records outside direct IRE pedagogy. It is therefore used only to refine transferability claims, identify contextual corroboration and specify future multilingual search needs; it does not change the 42-record included corpus. Appendix~A records this decision.

Study selection followed PRISMA 2020 guidance and earlier PRISMA reporting principles for transparent identification, screening, eligibility and inclusion decisions (Liberati et al., 2009, Moher et al., 2009, Page et al., 2021). Records identified through source searching were exported to reference management software. Duplicates were removed first within each source and then across the pooled set, with each retained unique record assigned to the first source in which it appeared for audit purposes. Titles and abstracts were screened against the inclusion criteria, after which full texts of potentially relevant articles were assessed for eligibility. Reference lists of included articles were also hand-searched to identify additional relevant studies. Figure~\ref{fig.prisma} visualises the selection process. No prospective external protocol registration was completed for this review. To compensate for this limitation, the search string, eligibility criteria, extraction fields, appraisal logic, evidence-use protocol, and sensitivity checks are reported explicitly so that screening and synthesis decisions remain auditable.

\subsection{Analytical Lens for the Synthesis}

After the review boundaries were set, the analysis needed a lens that could follow how religious knowledge moves across classrooms, institutions, communities, and digital platforms. Rather than forcing the material into one theory, the synthesis was organised around four connected lenses.

First, the review reads digital IRE as part of a networked knowledge ecology. In this setting, institutional authority, peer circulation, platform visibility, and identity performance interact (Castells, 2010, Hefner, 2007, Zaman, 2004). Religious authority is therefore negotiated across social, technological, and epistemic settings, not simply passed down through formal institutions.

Second, the pedagogical lens treats learning as a mediated process. Students interpret religious knowledge with teachers, texts, communities, platforms, and peers (Egan, 2010, Vygotsky, 1978, Schunk, 2012). Religious literacy is not simple information acquisition. It requires source interpretation, authority recognition, truth-claim evaluation, and ethical action in plural digital environments (Gilster, 1997, Goleman, 1995, Moore, 2013, van de Oudeweetering and Voogt, 2018).

Third, the Islamic epistemic lens defines authenticity as disciplined religious discernment, not personal sincerity alone. Reliable knowledge in IRE is mediated through recognised scholarship, textual reasoning, ethical character, and communal responsibility. Its classroom equivalents include \textit{tabayyun}, or verification before accepting and circulating claims, \textit{adab al-`ilm}, or proper conduct in seeking and sharing knowledge, attention to chains and contexts of authority, and moral orientation toward public benefit. These principles do not reject digital tools. Instead, they provide criteria for evaluating, contextualising, and ethically using digital religious content (Hefner, 2007, Nasr, 1996, Ramadan, 2009, Zaman, 2004).

Fourth, the applied lens connects digital IRE with teacher knowledge, instructional design, authentic assessment, ethical implementation, and educational change. Teacher expertise is pedagogical, theological, technological, and contextual, not merely technical (Fullan, 2007, Shulman, 1987). Strong digital IRE therefore connects digital affordances with Islamic epistemic discipline, moral formation, and authentic assessment (Keller, 2010, Mayer, 2020, Wiggins, 1998). Table~\ref{tab.conceptual-architecture} summarises these four lenses.

Because authenticity can mean different things in religious education, the review uses a controlled terminology. Doctrinal reliability refers to whether a claim is grounded in qualified scholarship and responsible textual reasoning. Pedagogical authenticity refers to whether learning tasks require meaningful religious judgement rather than superficial digital activity. Identity expression refers to how learners perform, narrate, and negotiate Muslim belonging online. Practice authenticity refers to whether institutional and classroom practices remain ethically responsible, context-sensitive, and accountable to public benefit. This distinction prevents the synthesis from treating doctrinal correctness, educational genuineness, identity performance, and institutional practice as the same construct.

Data extraction used a standardised form developed for this review. Extracted variables included author and year, country context, educational level, study design, evidence type, digital technologies examined, pedagogical approaches, key findings related to authority and authenticity, limitations and recommendations. The extracted material was organised in comparison matrices to support transparent coding, memoing, and iterative interpretation, consistent with qualitative data management principles (Bazeley and Jackson, 2013, Creswell and Poth, 2018). Extraction and checking were conducted through author-team review and audit memoing; uncertain classifications were resolved through documented discussion. Because the corpus combined primary studies and review-level evidence, no pooled reliability statistic is reported and no claim is made that an externally registered dual-screening protocol was completed.

An audit trail recorded four decision points for each included record, covering eligibility, evidence type, quality appraisal and theme use. This made it possible to distinguish records that shaped a theme from records that only contextualised a theme. Table~\ref{tab.audit-trail} summarises the audit logic.

Quality appraisal was guided by design-appropriate domains drawn from Joanna Briggs Institute critical appraisal guidance rather than by applying a single checklist to all records (Joanna Briggs Institute, 2017). Qualitative studies, quantitative observational studies, quasi-experimental studies, mixed-methods studies, bibliometric studies, and systematic reviews were appraised against domains appropriate to their design. The appraisal considered clarity of aims, methodological congruence, sampling or corpus transparency, data collection, analysis, findings, ethical considerations, limitations, and review reproducibility where applicable. This approach reflects wider calls for reviews that are explicit, reproducible, and resistant to selection bias (Haddaway et al., 2015, Petticrew and Roberts, 2006). Lower-confidence records were retained only for contextual interpretation and were not allowed to determine a theme unless corroborated by higher-confidence evidence.

Because the corpus combines primary empirical studies, systematic reviews, bibliometric studies, critical reviews and conceptual sources, evidence was not treated as interchangeable. The synthesis applied an evidence-use protocol to prevent review-level sources from being counted as primary outcome evidence and to prevent conceptual sources from being used as direct empirical support. Table~\ref{tab.evidence-use-protocol} summarises how each evidence type was used.

\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

To reduce double-counting, a theme was treated as robust only when it appeared across more than one evidence stream or when one high-confidence evidence stream was corroborated by independent conceptual, methodological or contextual support. Quantitative indicators from individual sources were therefore not pooled and were not used as prevalence estimates or meta-analytic effects.

A qualitative sensitivity check was then conducted in three ways. First, theme claims were re-read using only high- and moderate-confidence evidence to ensure that contextual sources did not determine a finding. Second, review-level records were used as corroborating evidence rather than as primary outcome evidence. Third, claims were checked against the geographic concentration of the corpus; when evidence outside Southeast Asia was thin, findings were framed as transferable questions rather than global conclusions. This sensitivity check retained Themes 1, 2 and 4 as strongly convergent, while treating Theme 3 as important but less empirically secure because student voice and longitudinal identity evidence remain limited.

Data analysis followed a thematic approach for synthesising mixed qualitative, quantitative, and review-level literature (Dixon-Woods et al., 2005, Sandelowski and Barroso, 2007, Thomas and Harden, 2008). The analytical framework was developed iteratively, beginning with broad coding of extracted data to identify preliminary patterns. Codes were refined through matrix comparison and analytic memoing into categories that remained close to the corpus (Bazeley and Jackson, 2013). The process followed Braun and Clarke's (2006) six phases, moving from familiarisation and initial coding to theme searching, theme review, theme definition, naming, and report production. Initial codes included authority fragmentation, digital literacy, hybrid pedagogy, identity negotiation, algorithmic influence, and teacher competence. These were grouped into interpretative categories and refined into four overarching themes. To enhance rigour and transparency, an audit trail recorded the evolution of codes and themes, decisions about theme development, and revisions to disputed codes and evidence-use decisions. The final synthesis was narrative, allowing findings to be interpreted across diverse methodologies and educational settings (Petticrew and Roberts, 2006).

\section{Results}

Source searching yielded 1,842 records. After duplicate removal, 1,246 unique records remained for screening. Title and abstract screening eliminated 1,082 records that did not meet the inclusion criteria, primarily because they focused on non-digital contexts of Islamic Religious Education or did not provide primary or review-level evidence relevant to pedagogy. This left 164 records for full-text assessment. During full-text review, 122 articles were excluded. These consisted of 38 articles with a non-educational focus, 27 with insufficient primary or review-level evidence, 25 in non-Islamic religious education contexts, 17 outside the 2015--2025 timeframe, and 15 outside the English or Indonesian language criteria. Ultimately, 42 records met all inclusion criteria and were included in the integrative review.

\subsection{Thematic Analysis Results}

Thematic analysis produced four interlocking themes, namely fragmented religious authority, critical digital literacy, Generation Z identity formation, and the pedagogical, ethical, and institutional conditions required for responsible IRE practice.

\subsubsection{Theme 1. Fragmentation of Religious Authority in Digital Contexts}

Theme 1 concerns IRE's response to fragmented religious authority. Across the corpus, digital environments are depicted as loosening older hierarchies of religious knowledge. Learners encounter teachers and scholars alongside online preachers, influencers, peers, and algorithmic systems. Credibility is therefore negotiated through visibility, repetition, and social endorsement as well as formal qualification (Samudra et al., 2024, Muhamad Arif et al., 2025).

A related shift is the rise of algorithmic authority. Recommendation systems shape which religious content becomes visible, repeated, and normalised (Miftachul Huda et al., 2024, Saidah et al., 2024). Several studies suggest that learners may prioritise accessible or recommended content over scholarly sources, creating opaque pathways to religious understanding (Istiani and Islamy, 2025). The corpus also records active contestation, as scholars and teachers challenge unreliable online claims while also entering the same digital spaces to reassert credible guidance (Efendi et al., 2025, Firdaus and Fauziyah, 2025, Hasanah and Permana, 2025). The supplementary Arabic-context scoping check pointed in the same direction by identifying virtual religious authority and micro-celebrity da'i as contextual examples of how visibility and platform circulation can reshape perceived religious legitimacy (Baidawi, 2025). Because this record was identified through supplementary scoping rather than the core PRISMA pool, it is treated as contextual corroboration only. \subsubsection{Theme 2. Critical Digital Literacy as a Pedagogical Imperative}

Theme 2 shows that disciplined religious understanding depends on critical digital literacy rather than access to digital resources alone. Across the corpus, effective frameworks combine source evaluation, algorithmic awareness, contextual understanding, cross-verification, and ethical online engagement (Adima et al., 2024, Muhamad Arif et al., 2025). Students trained in these competencies were described as better able to identify reliable religious sources and resist uncritical acceptance of online claims (Saidah et al., 2024).

Hybrid models were most persuasive when they avoided both technological determinism and rigid traditionalism. Examples include digital \textit{tadabbur}, problem-based digital learning, and classroom designs that connect digital tools with classical scholarship (Hasanah and Permana, 2025, Sapitri et al., 2025, Wa Ode Kurniati, 2025). Participatory approaches also support pedagogical authenticity by moving learners from teacher-centred reception toward collaborative meaning-making, structured digital \textit{majlis}, student-led projects, and guided peer inquiry (Efendi et al., 2025, Firdaus and Fauziyah, 2025, Fitriani et al., 2025). Several records further call for assessment that evaluates digital religious competence alongside content knowledge (Arif et al., 2025, Muksin and Afandi, 2025, Sarbini et al., 2025). \subsubsection{Theme 3. Identity Formation Challenges among Generation Z}

Theme 3 shifts from sources of knowledge to the formation of the learner. The evidence suggests that Muslim students negotiate religious identity by integrating, separating, or prioritising family, school, scholarly, peer, and digital influences (Efendi et al., 2025, Hasanah and Permana, 2025, Sapitri et al., 2025). These negotiations often produce hybrid religious identities rather than a simple transfer of institutional norms (boyd, 2014, Prensky, 2001).

Algorithmic environments complicate this process. Echo chambers can reinforce particular self-understandings through content curation (Miftachul Huda et al., 2024, Muhamad Arif et al., 2025, Saidah et al., 2024), while stronger critical digital literacy appears to help students resist identity narrowing (Adima et al., 2024, Muhamad Arif et al., 2025). The corpus also identifies emerging forms of digital religious expression, including visible practice, connective belonging through online community, and educational sharing of religious knowledge (Firdaus and Fauziyah, 2025, Fitriani et al., 2025, Sapitri et al., 2025). The review treats learner exposure to multiple identity-forming environments as a supported pedagogical concern, treats algorithmic influence on belonging and self-presentation as an interpretive pattern, and treats long-term identity transformation as a research agenda rather than as an established finding. This distinction preserves the theme's educational importance while preventing indirect and youth-focused evidence from being converted into broad prevalence claims.

\subsubsection{Theme 4. Pedagogical, Ethical, and Institutional Implications for IRE Practice}

Theme 4 extends the synthesis to practice and governance. The corpus repeatedly emphasises teacher professional development for the digital dimensions of IRE, including source evaluation, algorithmic awareness, online facilitation, ethical guidance, and the judicious use of technology (Erihadiana et al., 2025, Muhamad Arif et al., 2025, Wa Ode Kurniati, 2025). Teachers therefore need professional learning that moves beyond technical training toward pedagogical and theological discernment (Muhamad Arif et al., 2025, Shulman, 1987).

At curriculum level, hybrid designs should connect traditional Islamic knowledge with digital literacy, critical thinking, source verification, digital ethics, and guided engagement with online religious materials (Firdaus and Fauziyah, 2025, Muksin and Afandi, 2025, Sapitri et al., 2025). Such integration can increase engagement while preserving educational rigour (Erihadiana et al., 2025). At institutional level, the literature calls for protocols on technology integration, approved resources, misinformation response, inclusive access, privacy, ethical platform use, and digital-traditional balance (Arif et al., 2025, Hasanah and Permana, 2025, Pananrangi et al., 2025, Sarbini et al., 2025). \subsection{Debates and Contradictions in the Literature}

Several debates are central to digital IRE\@. One debate concerns authority and asks whether IRE should preserve hierarchical scholarly structures or respond to a more distributed religious field. Preservation-oriented studies emphasise the continuing importance of scholarly authority for doctrinal integrity and protection against misinterpretation (Berglund and Gent, 2018, Muhamad Arif et al., 2025). Studies of social media and algorithmic authority, however, suggest that the field has already changed. Students now need guidance for navigating multiple and competing sources, not simply reminders to trust inherited hierarchies (Samudra et al., 2024, Saidah et al., 2024).

A second debate concerns the balance between critical digital literacy and traditional content knowledge. Digital literacy-oriented studies argue that students cannot navigate online religious landscapes without explicit instruction in source evaluation, algorithmic awareness, and ethical engagement (Adima et al., 2024, Muhamad Arif et al., 2025, van de Oudeweetering and Voogt, 2018). Content-focused studies caution that media competence without religious depth may produce learners who can evaluate formats but lack Qur'anic, hadith, fiqh, akhlaq, and historical reasoning (Firdaus and Fauziyah, 2025, Sarbini et al., 2025). The synthesis supports integration rather than substitution.

A third debate concerns generational difference. Younger learners often show greater comfort with digital sources, while teachers and older community members may retain stronger allegiance to institutional authority (Efendi et al., 2025, Hasanah and Permana, 2025). Classroom tension emerges when these groups hold different assumptions about legitimate religious knowledge. Yet the evidence also suggests that age is not the sole explanation. Digital literacy, family and community context, and prior educational experience mediate how learners judge credibility.

A fourth debate concerns algorithms. Some evidence frames recommendation systems as a threat because they privilege visibility, attention, and engagement over scholarly credibility (Miftachul Huda et al., 2024, Saidah et al., 2024). Other evidence suggests that algorithms amplify existing patterns of content consumption, making student formation and critical evaluation central to any educational response (Samudra et al., 2024, Muhamad Arif et al., 2025). In either interpretation, algorithmic literacy becomes necessary for religious education.

A fifth debate concerns whether post-truth should be understood mainly as epistemic decline, democratisation of expertise, or a crisis of disciplined verification. McIntyre (2018) and Lewandowsky et al. (2017) emphasise misinformation, motivated reasoning and weakened public standards of evidence, while Fuller (2018) treats post-truth as a struggle over who has power to define knowledge. IRE cannot simply import either position. Islamic education requires a stronger normative account of truth, knowledge, certainty and responsibility, so post-truth is interpreted here through \textit{haqq}, \textit{haqiqa}, \textit{ilm}, \textit{yaqin}, \textit{zann}, \textit{tabayyun}, and adab. This reframes the issue from generic media scepticism to disciplined religious verification.

Taken together, these debates point to a balanced pedagogical position. Effective IRE must acknowledge fragmented authority while remaining connected to scholarship, teach digital literacy without weakening content knowledge, recognise generational difference without reducing it to age, and pair algorithmic awareness with source evaluation and ethical judgement. \section{Discussion}

\subsection{Synthesis of the Evidence}

Evidence indicates that digital transformation has altered IRE at three connected levels, namely the circulation of religious authority, the pedagogical mediation of knowledge, and the formation of Muslim learner identity. Traditional authority has not disappeared. It now works within a crowded ecology where scholars, teachers, peers, influencers, search engines, recommender systems, generative AI, and platform metrics all shape what learners encounter as religious knowledge (Istiani and Islamy, 2025, Samudra et al., 2024, van de Oudeweetering and Voogt, 2018). Together, the four themes are mutually reinforcing. Fragmented authority creates the need for critical digital literacy, digital literacy supports identity negotiation, and all three require institutional and pedagogical responses.

Authority fragmentation is the most consistently convergent finding across the reviewed evidence streams. Scholars and educational institutions remain important, but learners increasingly encounter them alongside algorithmically amplified content and online personalities (Samudra et al., 2024, Muhamad Arif et al., 2025). This extends earlier accounts of Islamic educational authority and modern educational challenge (Hefner, 2007, Nasr, 1996, Zaman, 2004) by showing that the contemporary problem is not only ideological plurality but also platform-mediated visibility (Berglund and Gent, 2018, Sahin, 2018). Authority is now a question not only of who is qualified to speak, but also of which voices become visible, repeatable, and socially persuasive (Istiani and Islamy, 2025).

Critical digital literacy is also necessary for religious learning in post-truth conditions. It links source evaluation, cross-verification, contextual interpretation, algorithmic awareness, and ethical engagement (Adima et al., 2024, Muhamad Arif et al., 2025, van de Oudeweetering and Voogt, 2018). This supports a view of religious literacy as interpretative judgement rather than information recall (Moore, 2013). Digital literacy in IRE must therefore be grounded in Islamic epistemic discipline, because the ability to identify misinformation is inseparable from the ability to recognise reliable religious reasoning (Lewandowsky et al., 2017, Nasr, 1996, Ramadan, 2009). In this sense, digital literacy becomes a contemporary pedagogical expression of \textit{tabayyun}. Learners pause, verify source credibility, examine context, compare interpretations, and consider the ethical consequences of sharing religious claims.

Hybrid pedagogy offers a route beyond the false opposition between traditionalism and technological enthusiasm. Digital tools improve engagement when subordinated to clear religious, pedagogical, and ethical aims (Sapitri et al., 2025, Wa Ode Kurniati, 2025). Technology is educationally valuable when it supports textual encounter, guided inquiry, collaborative reflection, and authentic assessment. It is pedagogically weak when it merely accelerates access to uncurated content (Erihadiana et al., 2025). Such designs resonate with accounts of cognitive tools and educational change because they organise digital resources around disciplined inquiry and sustainable institutional practice (Egan, 2010, Fullan, 2007).

Finally, identity formation is neither simple institutional transmission nor passive absorption of digital content. Generation Z Muslim learners negotiate identity across family, school, mosque, peer networks, and platforms (boyd, 2014, Prensky, 2001). The strongest defensible claim is pedagogical rather than predictive: IRE should help learners interpret these competing influences responsibly. A more cautious interpretive claim is that algorithmic exposure may shape belonging, self-presentation, and credibility judgements when digital participation is detached from scholarship and ethical reflection. The review does not claim to establish long-term identity effects. Because the process involves imagination, judgement, and affective self-regulation, IRE should treat identity formation as a guided educational concern rather than a private psychological process (Egan, 2010, Goleman, 1995).

\subsection{Theoretical Contribution}

This review offers a layered theoretical account of IRE in post-truth digital conditions. At the broadest level, it positions IRE within a networked knowledge ecology (Castells, 2010, Hefner, 2007), showing that authority challenges are structural rather than incidental. They arise from interactions among platforms, social circulation, institutional trust, and learner agency (Istiani and Islamy, 2025, Samudra et al., 2024). At the middle level, it advances the concept of digital religious authority to capture the interplay of scholarly legitimacy, institutional trust, influencer visibility, peer endorsement, and algorithmic amplification (Berglund and Gent, 2018, Sahin, 2018). At the Islamic pedagogical level, it defines authenticity through disciplined verification, adab, contextual reasoning, and ethical responsibility rather than popularity, immediacy, or performative online piety. At the applied level, it links source evaluation, algorithmic and generative-AI awareness, contextual interpretation, ethical engagement, and traditional religious literacy into a pedagogical account of digital religious discernment (Adima et al., 2024, van de Oudeweetering and Voogt, 2018). \subsection{Implications for Practice, Curriculum and Policy}

Teachers need professional competence that extends beyond technical familiarity with platforms. IRE teachers need pedagogical content knowledge that includes source evaluation, algorithmic awareness, online facilitation, and ethical guidance (Floridi, 2023, Shulman, 1987). Teacher development should include supervised practice in evaluating online religious claims, designing hybrid learning sequences, assessing digital religious judgement, and guiding students as they negotiate traditional and digital influences (Muhamad Arif et al., 2025). Sustainable implementation also requires professional learning cultures and change leadership, not one-off technology training (Fullan, 2007).

Curriculum developers should embed digital literacy across IRE rather than add it as a separate technology unit. Qur'anic studies, hadith, fiqh, akhlaq, Islamic history, and contemporary issues can each include tasks that ask students to evaluate sources, compare interpretations, identify misinformation, and articulate ethical responses (Adima et al., 2024, Muksin and Afandi, 2025, Sapitri et al., 2025). This integration preserves the integrity of Islamic knowledge while preparing students for digital religious life (Erihadiana et al., 2025). It also frames curriculum design as cognitive, ethical, and affective formation, requiring tasks that cultivate disciplined understanding, empathy, and responsible moral action (Egan, 2010, Goleman, 1995, Ramadan, 2009).

Operationally, curriculum tasks should build five Islamic epistemic safeguards into learning activities. These safeguards are \textit{tabayyun} for verification of source and claim, authority qualification for asking who is speaking or generating an answer and with what scholarly competence, contextual interpretation for reading texts with attention to genre, audience, and legal-ethical scope, \textit{adab} in disagreement and online sharing, and public-benefit reasoning for judging whether digital circulation serves learning, harm reduction, and moral responsibility. Together, they translate classical concerns for reliable knowledge and ethical reform into assessable digital learning behaviours (Nasr, 1996, Ramadan, 2009, Zaman, 2004).

Educational institutions, including schools, madrasah, and pesantren, should establish clear policies for digital IRE\@. These policies should address resource selection, classroom platform use, privacy, online discussion ethics, responses to misinformation, and responsible AI use (Arif et al., 2025, Pananrangi et al., 2025, Sarbini et al., 2025). They should not function as prohibition alone, but as positive frameworks for guided, responsible, and authentic engagement (Pananrangi et al., 2025, Sarbini et al., 2025). Because educational change is social and organisational, policy should be accompanied by shared leadership, teacher collaboration, and periodic implementation review (Fullan, 2007).

Implementation also requires attention to barriers that are often invisible in conceptual recommendations. In resource-limited pesantren or madrasah, the five safeguards can be introduced through low-bandwidth or offline activities, such as printed source-comparison tasks, teacher-curated screenshots, and classroom verification exercises before platform-based assignments. Teacher resistance should be addressed through co-designed professional learning rather than one-off technical training. Parental concerns about online religious content can be addressed through transparent platform rules, home-school communication, and community-based digital literacy sessions. Assessment validity requires rubrics that evaluate evidence of verification, contextual reasoning, ethical sharing, and reflection, not merely the ability to use a device. Digital divide issues require institutional plans for shared access, inclusive materials, and alternatives for learners with limited connectivity.

Policymakers and Muslim communities should treat digital IRE as a shared educational responsibility. Formal institutions cannot address fragmented authority alone because students encounter religious content across homes, mosques, peer groups, and social media (boyd, 2014, Selod, 2018). Community-based digital literacy, intergenerational dialogue, and partnerships with credible religious educators are therefore necessary complements to school-based reform (Berglund and Gent, 2018, Sahin, 2018). Policymakers should develop standards for digital IRE, update teacher certification requirements, and support further research into effective approaches (Lewandowsky et al., 2017).

\subsection{Limitations and Future Research}

Several limitations should be noted. First, the evidence base is geographically concentrated, with strong representation from Indonesia and Malaysia. This reflects important research productivity in Southeast Asia, but it means that the findings should be read as Southeast Asia-weighted rather than globally representative of all Muslim educational contexts (Berglund and Gent, 2018, Selod, 2018). Second, the primary review corpus is limited to English and Indonesian publications. A supplementary Arabic-context scoping check was inspected after the main synthesis and helped identify relevant English-language Arabic or Arab-context debates on student voice, identity and virtual religious authority (Baidawi, 2025, Kubow, 2023). However, it does not remove the main language limitation: Arabic-language scholarship and Arabic databases were not systematically searched, and Turkish, Persian, Urdu, Malay, French and other work may also address similar questions through different epistemic vocabularies. This language boundary is therefore handled as a transferability constraint rather than hidden as a minor search decision. Third, methodological diversity supports rich synthesis but limits causal inference. Fourth, Theme 3 on identity formation is theoretically and pedagogically important, but it remains the least secure theme because direct student-voice and longitudinal IRE evidence are thinner than the evidence for authority, literacy and institutional practice. The supplementary Arabic-context scoping check strengthens the rationale for this research agenda but does not convert identity claims into stable prevalence claims about all Muslim learners. Fifth, no prospective external protocol registration was completed. This cannot be corrected after screening and synthesis have already occurred; Appendix~A therefore reconstructs the operational protocol and audit fields used in the completed review, without representing this as prospective registration. Sixth, rapid technological change means that findings about specific platforms may require frequent updating, especially as artificial intelligence reshapes how learners search for and evaluate religious knowledge (Floridi, 2023, Istiani and Islamy, 2025).

Future research should prioritise longitudinal studies of religious identity formation, comparative research across Islamic educational traditions, and empirical testing of digital religious literacy frameworks (Adima et al., 2024, Miftachul Huda et al., 2024). Further work is needed on artificial intelligence in IRE, algorithmic recommendation effects, teacher professional development, assessment validity, and policy implementation in resource-limited settings (Istiani and Islamy, 2025, Muhamad Arif et al., 2025). Student voice should be foregrounded through interviews, classroom ethnographies, learner diaries, participatory design studies and longitudinal follow-up, especially in relation to how learners judge authority, doctrinal reliability, pedagogical authenticity, identity expression and ethical responsibility online. Future multilingual reviews should add Arabic search strings and Arabic databases or repositories, then extend the protocol to Turkish, Persian, Urdu, Malay, French and other relevant languages where Islamic education scholarship is active. Future systematic reviews should also register a protocol before screening begins, preferably through an open repository or review registry appropriate to education research. Cross-cultural and multilingual studies across Southeast Asian, Middle Eastern, European, African, and North American contexts would strengthen global understanding of digital IRE challenges and responses, especially if they include Arabic and other Islamic scholarly languages (Berglund and Gent, 2018, Sahin, 2018).

\section{Conclusion}

This systematic integrative review shows that Islamic Religious Education in the post-truth digital era operates within a fragmented authority landscape shaped by scholars, institutions, influencers, peer communities, algorithms, generative AI, and platform visibility. Its central task is not to choose between tradition and technology, but to form learners who can recognise qualified authority, evaluate digital religious claims, read texts contextually, practise \textit{adab} online, and act with ethical responsibility. The review contributes an integrated account of digital religious authority, distinguishes doctrinal reliability, pedagogical authenticity, identity expression and practice authenticity, and shows that hybrid pedagogy is educationally valuable when digital tools serve textual understanding, character formation, and authentic assessment. Practice therefore requires teacher development, curriculum redesign, institutional policy, and community engagement that support source verification, algorithmic and AI awareness, digital ethics, guided participation, and context-sensitive implementation. Because the evidence base is Southeast Asia-weighted, the framework should be transferred carefully and tested comparatively in other Muslim educational contexts. In this sense, digital IRE is not a compromise with digital culture. It is a necessary educational response to the conditions in which Muslim learners now encounter, contest, and live religious knowledge.
