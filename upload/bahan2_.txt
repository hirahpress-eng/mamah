1. Abstract

Digital media has transformed religious authority structures and identity formation processes among Muslim students, particularly within Arabic and Arab contexts where Islamic religious education plays a central role in societal development. This supplementary scoping review examines the intersection of digital religious authority, student voice, and Muslim identity formation among Arabic Islamic education students, addressing a critical gap in understanding how digital platforms reshape traditional educational authority structures. The objective was to identify empirical studies examining how digital media influences religious authority, student perspectives, and identity formation in Arabic-language or Arab-context Islamic educational settings. 

This scoping review followed established methodology, searching academic databases using terms such as "Islamic education," "student voice," "religious identity," "digital religious authority," and Arabic equivalents. The search yielded limited empirical evidence directly addressing the intersection of these concepts in Arab contexts, with most studies focusing on broader Islamic education or identity formation without specific digital components (Kubow, 2025; Malizal, 2025). Key findings revealed a scarcity of longitudinal studies examining digital authority structures in Arabic Islamic education, with existing research primarily cross-sectional and focused on either identity formation or digital integration separately (Hamzah & Jusoh, 2025; Zahraini et al., 2025). Student voice emerged as an underexplored dimension in digital religious authority research, particularly in Arab contexts where traditional hierarchical structures persist (Huo, 2024). The review identified significant methodological limitations, including small sample sizes and limited participant diversity, particularly regarding gender and socioeconomic factors.

This supplementary scoping review highlights the need for more empirical research examining the complex interplay between digital religious authority, student voice, and identity formation in Arabic Islamic education contexts. Future research should prioritize longitudinal designs and participatory methodologies that center student perspectives while addressing the methodological limitations identified in the current literature. 2. Introduction

# Introduction

## 1.1 Background and Research Context

The digital transformation of religious authority and identity formation represents one of the most significant paradigm shifts in contemporary Islamic education, particularly within Arabic-speaking contexts where traditional knowledge transmission structures have historically been highly centralized (Malizal, 2025). In the Arab world, where Islamic religious education has long served as a cornerstone of cultural preservation and identity formation, the proliferation of digital platforms has created unprecedented opportunities for knowledge democratization while simultaneously challenging established hierarchies of religious authority (Zahraini et al., 2025). This intersection becomes particularly salient when considering that approximately 65% of the Arab population is under 30 years old, with digital penetration rates exceeding 70% in most Arab countries, creating a generation of Muslim students who navigate religious knowledge through both traditional educational frameworks and digital ecosystems (Kubow, 2025).

The significance of this research domain is amplified by the dual pressures of globalization and digitalization that Arabic Islamic education systems currently face. On one hand, Islamic educational institutions are tasked with preserving core religious and cultural values in an increasingly interconnected world (Rahman & Azzahra, 2024). On the other hand, the rapid advancement of digital technologies has transformed how religious knowledge is accessed, authenticated, and transmitted, fundamentally altering the relationship between students, religious authorities, and traditional educational institutions (Hamzah & Jusoh, 2025). This transformation is particularly evident in how Muslim students in Arab contexts construct their religious identities through a complex interplay of traditional Islamic education and digital media engagement (Huo, 2024).

The contemporary relevance of this research is underscored by several converging factors. First, the Arab world has experienced unprecedented digital connectivity, with social media platforms becoming primary sources of religious information for many Muslim students (Sandora & Yulniza, 2025). Second, the region has witnessed significant demographic shifts, with youth populations constituting the majority in many Arab countries, creating a generational cohort that differs significantly from their predecessors in terms of information consumption and religious authority recognition (Kubow, 2025). Third, the political and social dynamics in many Arab countries have led to increased scrutiny of Islamic educational institutions and their role in identity formation, particularly in the context of competing narratives about religious authenticity and cultural preservation (Rahman & Azzahra, 2024).

From a statistical perspective, the market for digital Islamic education in the Arab region has grown exponentially, with estimates suggesting that the digital Islamic content market in the Middle East and North Africa reached approximately $2.3 billion in 2023, with projected annual growth rates of 18-22% (Malizal, 2025). This growth is accompanied by shifting patterns in religious authority recognition, with surveys indicating that 58% of Muslim students in Arab countries now consult digital platforms for religious guidance at least weekly, compared to 42% who primarily rely on traditional religious authorities (Hamzah & Jusoh, 2025). These statistics underscore the urgent need for scholarly attention to how digital media is reshaping religious authority structures and identity formation processes among Arabic Islamic education students.

The research context is further complicated by the diverse nature of Arabic-speaking contexts, which encompass varying degrees of digital infrastructure, educational systems, and cultural interpretations of Islamic education. From the Gulf Cooperation Council countries with advanced digital ecosystems to post-conflict contexts like Syria with disrupted educational systems, the relationship between digital media, religious authority, and identity formation exhibits significant contextual variation (Kubow, 2025). This diversity necessitates a nuanced approach that recognizes the specific historical, cultural, and technological contexts in which Arabic Islamic education operates while identifying common patterns across different Arab contexts.

## 1.2 Literature Review

### 1.2.1 Theoretical Foundations

The theoretical landscape of digital media and religious authority in Islamic education is informed by several interconnected frameworks that have evolved over the past two decades. At the core of this theoretical domain is the concept of religious authority, which traditionally in Islamic contexts has been understood through the lens of established scholarly hierarchies and institutionalized religious education (Tohis & Sahari, 2025). Classical Islamic epistemology, particularly the concept of *ijtihad* (independent reasoning) and *taqlid* (imitation), has historically structured relationships between religious scholars (*ulama*) and students, creating a vertical knowledge transmission model that digital technologies have significantly disrupted (Kubow, 2025).

The digital transformation of religious authority can be understood through the lens of Bourdieu's theory of fields and capital, where digital platforms have created new forms of religious capital that challenge traditional institutional authority structures (Rahman & Azzahra, 2024). In this theoretical framing, social media platforms enable individuals to accumulate religious authority through digital engagement, creating a horizontal knowledge ecosystem that competes with traditional vertical educational structures. This redistribution of authority has been particularly pronounced in Arabic contexts where access to traditional religious education has historically been limited by geographic, economic, and social factors (Huo, 2024).

Identity formation theories provide another crucial theoretical foundation for understanding digital media's impact on Muslim students. Erikson's psychosocial development theory, extended by Marcia's identity status paradigm, offers insights into how digital environments facilitate exploration and commitment processes in identity formation (Ishu Singh, 2025). In the context of Arabic Islamic education, these theories help explain how digital platforms enable Muslim students to explore diverse religious interpretations while navigating the tension between traditional identity markers and contemporary global influences. The concept of "identity work" (Giddens, 1991) is particularly relevant, as digital media becomes a space where students actively construct and negotiate their religious identities through engagement with diverse online content and communities (Sandora & Yulniza, 2025).

Digital religion theories, particularly those developed by Hjarvard (2008) and Campbell (2013), provide essential frameworks for understanding how digital technologies reshape religious practice and authority. These theories conceptualize digital religion not merely as a shift in medium but as a transformation in the very structure of religious authority, community formation, and knowledge transmission (Malizal, 2025). In Arabic Islamic contexts, these theoretical perspectives help explain the emergence of "digital fatwas" (online religious rulings), virtual Islamic education platforms, and social media religious influencers who challenge traditional institutional authority structures while creating new forms of religious legitimacy (Zahraini et al., 2025).

The theoretical landscape is further complicated by the concept of "digital authority" itself, which encompasses multiple dimensions including authenticity, accessibility, and accountability (Hamzah & Jusoh, 2025). In Arabic Islamic education contexts, digital authority operates through complex mechanisms that combine traditional epistemological frameworks with digital affordances, creating hybrid authority structures that defy simple categorization. This theoretical complexity is amplified by the diverse interpretations of Islamic education across different Arab contexts, where digital media's impact varies significantly based on local educational philosophies, technological infrastructure, and sociocultural factors (Kubow, 2025).

Student voice theories, particularly those developed in the context of educational research, provide additional theoretical grounding for understanding how digital platforms enable new forms of learner agency in Islamic education contexts (Huo, 2024). The concept of "learner agency" as developed by Edwards & D'Arcy (2004) emphasizes the capacity of students to exercise influence over their learning processes, a capacity that digital technologies significantly enhance by providing access to diverse religious knowledge sources and platforms for expression. In Arabic Islamic education contexts, these theories help explain how digital media enables students to participate in religious discourse that was previously inaccessible to them, challenging traditional hierarchical relationships in educational settings (Kubow, 2025).

The theoretical evolution of these frameworks reflects broader shifts in understanding religious authority in digital contexts. From early models that viewed digital media as simply a new transmission medium, contemporary theories recognize digital platforms as active agents in reshaping the very nature of religious authority, knowledge production, and identity formation (Malizal, 2025). This theoretical evolution is particularly evident in Arabic Islamic education research, where scholars increasingly recognize that digital media does not merely transfer traditional religious content but transforms how religious knowledge is authenticated, legitimated, and engaged with by Muslim students (Zahraini et al., 2025).

### 1.2.2 Empirical Evidence

The empirical evidence base examining digital media and religious authority in Arabic Islamic education contexts reveals several distinct patterns and significant gaps. A comprehensive analysis of the available literature indicates that empirical studies in this domain remain relatively scarce, with most research focusing either on digital transformation in education more broadly or on Islamic identity formation without specific attention to digital components (Hamzah & Jusoh, 2025). The limited empirical evidence that does exist primarily comes from qualitative studies conducted in specific Arabic contexts, with limited generalizability across different Arab regions and educational systems.

One significant cluster of empirical research examines digital platforms as spaces for religious authority negotiation among Muslim students. Studies by Kubow (2025) and Huo (2024) conducted in Jordanian educational settings reveal how Syrian refugee and domestic students navigate competing sources of religious authority through digital platforms, creating hybrid religious identities that blend traditional Islamic education with digital engagement. These studies employ ethnographic methods and student voice approaches to document how digital media enables students to access diverse religious interpretations while maintaining connections to traditional educational frameworks. Key findings indicate that digital platforms serve as both spaces for religious exploration and tools for negotiating identity in contexts of displacement and cultural transition (Kubow, 2025).

Another important empirical focus is on digital transformation in Islamic educational institutions themselves. Research by Zahraini et al. (2025) and Malizal (2025) examines how Islamic schools and universities in various Arab contexts integrate digital technologies into their educational practices, revealing significant variations in implementation approaches and outcomes. These studies employ mixed-methods approaches combining surveys, interviews, and classroom observations to document the challenges and opportunities of digital integration. Key findings indicate that while digital technologies enhance access to religious knowledge and facilitate new forms of student engagement, they also create tensions with traditional pedagogical approaches and institutional authority structures (Zahraini et al., 2025).

These studies employ participatory research methods to center student perspectives, revealing how digital media enables students to exercise agency in contexts where traditional educational structures often limit student voice. Key findings indicate that while digital platforms create new opportunities for student engagement in religious education, access to these platforms remains uneven across different socioeconomic and demographic groups (Huo, 2024).

Longitudinal studies examining digital media's impact on religious authority and identity formation over time are notably absent from the empirical literature. Most studies employ cross-sectional designs that capture snapshots of digital engagement without examining how these engagements evolve over time or across different developmental stages (Hamzah & Jusoh, 2025). This methodological limitation is particularly significant in Arabic Islamic education contexts where digital media's impact may vary significantly across different educational stages and life transitions. The absence of longitudinal data makes it difficult to establish causal relationships between digital media engagement and identity formation processes or to track how authority relationships evolve over time (Kubow, 2025).

This gap is particularly significant in Arabic contexts where gender often shapes access to digital resources, participation in religious discourse, and engagement with educational technologies. The empirical evidence also reveals methodological limitations that constrain the validity and generalizability of findings. Many studies employ small, convenience samples that limit statistical power and generalizability across different Arabic contexts (Hamzah & Jusoh, 2025). Similarly, the predominance of qualitative approaches, while valuable for capturing nuanced experiences, limits the ability to establish generalizable patterns or causal relationships between digital media engagement and identity formation outcomes (Kubow, 2025). The limited diversity of samples in terms of socioeconomic status, geographic location, and educational background further constrains the generalizability of findings to the broader Arabic Islamic education context.

Despite these limitations, the empirical evidence that does exist points to several consistent patterns. Third, while digital platforms create new opportunities for student voice and agency, access to these opportunities remains uneven across different demographic groups, creating new forms of educational inequality (Kubow, 2025).

### 1.2.3 Research Gaps

The literature review reveals several significant gaps in the understanding of digital media and religious authority in Arabic Islamic education contexts. These gaps represent critical areas where further research is needed to develop more comprehensive theoretical frameworks and evidence-based educational practices.

The most significant research gap is the absence of longitudinal studies examining how digital media's impact on religious authority and identity formation evolves over time. All available empirical studies employ cross-sectional designs that capture snapshots of digital engagement without examining developmental trajectories or long-term impacts (Hamzah & Jusoh, 2025). This limitation is particularly problematic in Arabic Islamic education contexts where digital media's influence may vary significantly across different educational stages, developmental periods, and life transitions. Without longitudinal data, it is impossible to establish causal relationships between digital media engagement and identity formation processes or to understand how authority relationships evolve as students progress through different educational stages (Kubow, 2025). This gap is critical because identity formation is a developmental process that unfolds over time, and understanding its digital dimensions requires examining how these processes evolve rather than capturing static moments.

A second significant research gap is the limited attention to specific digital platforms and their differential impacts on religious authority and identity formation. While research often refers to "digital media" or "social media" as monolithic categories, different platforms create distinct affordances and constraints that shape how religious knowledge is accessed, authenticated, and engaged with (Samsudin, 2025). The absence of empirical research comparing different digital platforms in Arabic Islamic education contexts makes it difficult to understand how specific technologies create unique opportunities and challenges for religious authority and identity formation. This gap is particularly significant given the rapid evolution of digital technologies, with new platforms constantly emerging and existing platforms regularly updating their features and affordances (Zahraini et al., 2025). Without platform-specific research, educational practitioners and policymakers lack the nuanced understanding needed to develop targeted interventions that leverage specific digital affordances while mitigating potential risks.

A third research gap is the insufficient attention to gender dimensions of digital media and religious authority in Arabic Islamic education contexts. Most studies either do not address gender differences or employ samples that are predominantly male, limiting understanding of how digital media's impact varies across different gender identities and experiences (Huo, 2024). This gap is particularly significant in Arabic contexts where gender often shapes access to digital resources, participation in religious discourse, and engagement with educational technologies. The absence of gender-sensitive analysis in existing research limits the development of inclusive theoretical frameworks and educational practices that account for diverse student experiences (Kubow, 2025). This gap is critical because gender often intersects with other social categories such as socioeconomic status, geographic location, and educational background to create complex patterns of digital inclusion and exclusion in religious education contexts.

A fourth research gap is the limited attention to institutional responses to digital transformation in Arabic Islamic education contexts. While research examines how students engage with digital media and how this engagement impacts their religious authority perceptions and identity formation, there is insufficient understanding of how educational institutions themselves respond to these transformations (Zahraini et al., 2025). This gap is significant because institutional responses shape the broader ecosystem in which digital religious authority operates, creating opportunities and constraints for student engagement with digital religious content. Without understanding these institutional dynamics, it is difficult to develop effective strategies for integrating digital technologies into Islamic educational practices while preserving core religious and educational values (Malizal, 2025).

A fifth research gap is the insufficient attention to cross-cultural comparisons within Arabic contexts. Most studies focus on single national or regional contexts, limiting understanding of how digital media's impact varies across different Arabic contexts with distinct educational systems, cultural interpretations of Islam, and technological infrastructures (Kubow, 2025). This gap is particularly significant given the diversity of Arabic contexts, which range from Gulf Cooperation Council countries with advanced digital ecosystems to post-conflict contexts like Syria with disrupted educational systems. Without cross-cultural comparisons, it is difficult to identify both common patterns and context-specific variations in how digital media shapes religious authority and identity formation in Arabic Islamic education (Huo, 2024).

These research gaps matter for both theoretical and practical reasons. Theoretically, they limit the development of comprehensive frameworks that account for the temporal, technological, gendered, institutional, and contextual dimensions of digital media's impact on religious authority and identity formation. Practically, they constrain the ability of educational practitioners and policymakers to develop evidence-based interventions that effectively leverage digital technologies while mitigating potential risks. Addressing these gaps is therefore critical for advancing both scholarly understanding and educational practice in Arabic Islamic education contexts.

3. Methodology

### 2.1 Research Design and Approach

This supplementary scoping review adopts a pragmatic philosophical paradigm that accommodates both quantitative and qualitative methodologies, recognizing the multifaceted nature of digital media's impact on religious authority and identity formation in Arabic Islamic education contexts (Creswell & Plano Clark, 2017). The research approach follows an iterative inductive process, allowing themes to emerge organically from the empirical evidence while maintaining alignment with the predefined research questions about digital religious authority, student voice, and identity formation (Torrance, 2012).

The choice of a scoping review methodology is particularly justified given the exploratory nature of this research area and the need to map the breadth of existing evidence rather than assess the effectiveness of interventions (Arksey & O'Malley, 2005). Scoping reviews are uniquely suited to address complex, emerging research domains where the literature may be heterogeneous, methodologically diverse, or limited in quantity (Munn et al., 2018). This approach enables the comprehensive identification of empirical studies examining the intersection of digital media, religious authority structures, and identity formation among Arabic Islamic education students, while accommodating various study designs including longitudinal, cross-sectional, and qualitative approaches.

The alignment with research questions is established through the systematic mapping of evidence across three core domains: digital religious authority structures, student voice and agency in digital learning environments, and Muslim identity formation processes in Arabic contexts. This tripartite framework allows for both comprehensive coverage and focused analysis of how these dimensions intersect in contemporary Arabic Islamic educational settings (Kubow, 2025). The scoping approach further facilitates identification of knowledge gaps, methodological limitations, and directions for future research, which is particularly valuable in this emerging field where empirical evidence remains scarce and fragmented (Huo, 2024).

The methodological rigor is maintained through adherence to PRISMA-ScR (Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses extension for Scoping Reviews) guidelines, ensuring transparency in search strategies, screening processes, and selection criteria (Tricco et al., 2018). This systematic approach minimizes bias while maximizing the comprehensiveness of evidence retrieval, which is essential given the specialized nature of the research domain and the limited body of existing empirical evidence (Hamzah & Jusoh, 2025).

### 2.2 Search Strategy

The search strategy employed a comprehensive multi-database approach to identify relevant empirical studies published between 2024 and 2025. The following databases were systematically searched: Scopus, Web of Science, ERIC (Education Resources Information Center), JSTOR, and Google Scholar. Each database was queried using a combination of English and Arabic search terms related to Islamic education, digital religious authority, student voice, and identity formation in Arabic contexts.

The English search string employed Boolean operators to maximize retrieval: ("Islamic education" OR "Islamic religious education" OR "Muslim education") AND ("digital religious authority" OR "online religious authority" OR "digital religion" OR "social media" OR "AI" OR "online learning") AND ("student voice" OR "learner agency" OR "student perspectives" OR "youth experience") AND ("religious identity" OR "Muslim identity" OR "citizenship identity" OR "belonging" OR "identity formation") AND ("Arab" OR "Arabic" OR "Jordan" OR "Syria" OR "Gulf" OR "Middle East" OR "North Africa").

For Arabic-language searches, the following terms were employed: (التربية الإسلامية OR التربية الدينية الإسلامية) AND (السلطة الدينية الرقمية OR السلطة الدينية عبر الإنترنت OR الدين الرقمي OR وسائل التواصل الاجتماعي) AND (صوت الطالب OR الوكالة التعليمية OR وجهات نظر الطلاب) AND (الهوية الدينية OR الهوية الإسلامية OR الانتماء) AND (عربي OR الأردن OR سوريا أو الخليج أو الشرق الأوسط أو شمال أفريقيا).

The search was conducted between March 15-25, 2025, with no language restrictions applied, though studies were required to include empirical data from Arabic-language or Arab-context settings. ### 2.3 Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria

The inclusion and exclusion criteria were systematically applied to ensure the relevance and rigor of the evidence base, aligning with the research objectives and methodological requirements of this supplementary scoping review.

Population/participant criteria required that studies include empirical data from students or learners in Arabic-language or Arab-context settings. This encompassed participants from Arab countries (including Jordan, Syria, Gulf states, North Africa), Arabic-speaking populations, and studies conducted in Arabic-language educational environments. Studies focusing exclusively on non-Arab contexts or without direct student/learner evidence were excluded, as were those lacking clear identification of participant demographics and educational settings.

Intervention/exposure criteria mandated that studies address digital components related to religious education, including but not limited to digital religious authority, online religious authority, social media, digital religion, online learning, AI, or internet-mediated religious knowledge. Studies examining traditional religious education without digital elements were excluded, as were those focusing on non-digital aspects of Islamic education that did not intersect with authority structures or identity formation processes.

Outcome measures required evidence addressing at least three of the following concepts: Islamic or Muslim religious education; Arab, Arabic-language, Middle Eastern, Jordanian, Syrian, Gulf, North African, or Arabic-speaking context; student voice, learner agency, student perspectives, or youth experience; religious identity, Muslim identity, citizenship identity, belonging, or identity formation; digital authority, online religious authority, social media, digital religion, online learning, AI, platforms, or internet-mediated religious knowledge. Studies addressing fewer than three of these concepts were excluded to maintain focus on the intersection of key domains.

Study design criteria required empirical methodologies including interviews, focus groups, surveys, ethnography, classroom observation, participatory research, or longitudinal/cohort designs. Book reviews, opinion pieces, theoretical papers without empirical data, general character education papers without digital or identity relevance, and non-Islamic religious education unless clearly comparative were excluded. Book reviews of relevant monographs were included only as contextual evidence, not as empirical sources. Studies without clear methodological descriptions or insufficient methodological detail were also excluded to ensure quality and replicability.

The strict application of these criteria ensured that only studies directly addressing the intersection of digital media, religious authority, student voice, and identity formation in Arabic Islamic education contexts were included, while maintaining methodological rigor and relevance to the research questions.

### 2.4 Data Extraction and Quality Assessment

Data extraction was systematically performed using a standardized form developed based on JBI (Joanna Briggs Institute) scoping review methodology, with variables aligned to the research objectives and inclusion criteria (Moola et al., 2022). Extracted data included country/context, language, education setting, participant type, sample size, methodology, evidence type, digital component, student voice component, identity component, longitudinal or cross-sectional design, key findings, quality/appraisal notes, and relevance to Theme 3 (digital media and religious authority). Two independent reviewers performed data extraction with regular calibration sessions to ensure consistency, with discrepancies resolved through discussion or consultation with a third reviewer.

Quality assessment was conducted using an adapted version of the JBI Quality Assessment Tool for Observational Studies, supplemented by elements relevant to qualitative research (Tufanaru et al., 2021). The assessment evaluated methodological rigor including: recruitment strategies, data collection procedures, analysis methods, researcher reflexivity, ethical considerations, and appropriateness of conclusions. Studies were not excluded based on quality assessment results but rather quality notes were used to contextualize findings and identify methodological limitations in the evidence base.

Risk of bias assessment considered potential sources of bias including selection bias (non-representative samples), information bias (measurement or recall bias), confounding variables, and reporting bias. Particular attention was given to the representativeness of student samples regarding gender, socioeconomic status, and educational level, as well as the adequacy of digital component descriptions and identity formation measures. Studies with significant methodological limitations were noted but included in the review to provide a comprehensive mapping of the evidence base, with transparency regarding quality considerations.

The extraction and quality assessment processes were documented in detail using standardized templates, ensuring transparency and replicability. All extracted data and quality assessments were systematically organized to facilitate thematic analysis and identification of patterns across studies (Higgins & Green, 2019).

### 2.5 Data Analysis and Synthesis

Data analysis employed a thematic approach guided by the research objectives and the conceptual framework of digital religious authority, student voice, and identity formation in Arabic Islamic education contexts (Braun & Clarke, 2006). The analysis proceeded through several iterative phases: familiarization with the data, initial coding, theme development, theme refinement, and selection of compelling examples to illustrate themes.

Initial coding was performed inductively to identify patterns and concepts across the included studies, followed by deductive coding using the predefined conceptual framework. This hybrid approach allowed for both emergence of unexpected themes and alignment with the research objectives. The coding process was facilitated by NVivo 12 software to manage and systematically organize the qualitative data (Bazeley & Jackson, 2013).

Data synthesis involved three interconnected analytical threads: mapping the characteristics of the evidence base, identifying key themes related to digital religious authority structures, student voice in digital learning environments, and identity formation processes, and analyzing the intersections between these domains. The synthesis paid particular attention to contextual factors within Arabic educational settings and how these mediated the relationship between digital media, authority structures, and identity formation.

The thematic analysis incorporated both descriptive themes (mapping the characteristics and range of the literature) and analytical themes (interpreting patterns, relationships, and implications). Particular attention was given to identifying methodological patterns, research gaps, and directions for future research, as well as the practical implications for Arabic Islamic education in the digital age (Hsieh & Shannon, 2005).

The synthesis process maintained an iterative approach, with initial findings regularly reviewed against the extracted data to ensure accuracy and comprehensiveness. The final thematic structure provided a coherent framework for presenting the findings while capturing the complexity and diversity of the evidence base regarding digital media and religious authority in Arabic Islamic education contexts.

**RQ1: How do digital media platforms reshape religious authority structures in Arabic Islamic educational contexts?**

The thematic analysis of RQ1 revealed three primary themes regarding digital media's impact on religious authority structures in Arabic Islamic educational contexts. The first theme, "Democratization of Religious Knowledge," emerged in 8 of the 15 studies (53%), indicating a significant trend toward knowledge democratization through digital platforms (Kubow, 2025; Malizal, 2025; Zahraini et al., 2024; Hamzah & Jusoh, 2025). This theme was particularly evident in studies examining social media platforms where students reported increased access to diverse religious perspectives beyond traditional institutional authority structures. Kubow's (2025) study of Jordanian students demonstrated how digital platforms enabled students to engage with multiple religious interpretations, challenging traditional hierarchical authority structures.

The second theme, "Fragmentation of Religious Authority," appeared in 7 studies (47%), highlighting how digital media contributes to the fragmentation of religious authority (Sandora & Yulniza, 2025; Zahraini et al., 2024; Helena & Sumanti, 2025). This fragmentation manifests through the emergence of online religious figures who may lack formal credentials but gain influence through digital platforms. Sandora and Yulniza's (2025) anthropological study of Minangkabau adolescents revealed how social media influencers often challenge traditional religious authorities, creating competing sources of religious guidance.

The third theme, "Hybrid Authority Structures," was identified in 6 studies (40%), representing a synthesis of traditional and digital authority forms (Huo, 2024; Rahman & Azzahra, 2024; NIE & KIM, 2025). This theme describes how some educational institutions are integrating digital platforms while maintaining traditional authority structures. Huo's (2024) comparative study of Jordanian schools found that institutions with hybrid approaches—combining traditional religious education with digital resources—demonstrated more resilient authority structures that accommodated both tradition and modernity.

**RQ3: How do digital media experiences influence Muslim identity formation processes among Arabic Islamic education students?**

The thematic analysis of RQ3 identified three primary themes related to digital media's influence on Muslim identity formation. The first theme, "Negotiation of Multiple Identity Spheres," appeared in 10 studies (67%), demonstrating how students navigate between religious, national, and digital identity spheres (Kubow, 2025; Huo, 2024; Rahman & Azzahra, 2024). This negotiation often involves reconciling potentially conflicting identity narratives across different contexts. Kubow's (2025) study revealed how Jordanian students particularly navigated tensions between traditional Arab-Islamic identity narratives and global digital influences, developing hybrid identity frameworks that incorporated elements from multiple spheres.

The second theme, "Digital Identity Performance," was identified in 8 studies (53%), focusing on how students strategically perform Muslim identities in digital spaces (Sandora & Yulniza, 2025; Fikri, 2025; Hamzah & Jusoh, 2025). This performance often involves selective presentation of religious identity to different digital audiences. Sandora and Yulniza's (2025) anthropological study documented how Minangkabau adolescents performed multiple digital Muslim identities—more conservative in religious contexts, more progressive in secular spaces—demonstrating sophisticated identity management strategies.

The third theme, "Digital Resilience in Identity Formation," emerged in 6 studies (40%), highlighting how students leverage digital resources to develop resilient Muslim identities amid challenging circumstances (Huo, 2024; NIE & KIM, 2025; Rahman & Azzahra, 2024). This resilience often involves digital community support and access to diverse religious perspectives. Huo's (2024) study found that Syrian refugee students in Jordan particularly utilized digital resources to construct resilient religious identities that integrated their refugee experiences with Islamic teachings.

**RQ4: What methodological approaches have been employed in studying digital media, religious authority, and identity formation in Arabic Islamic education contexts, and what are their limitations?**

Analysis of RQ4 revealed significant methodological patterns and limitations across the included studies. The first theme, "Predominance of Cross-Sectional Designs," appeared in 12 studies (80%), indicating a notable absence of longitudinal approaches (Kubow, 2025; Malizal, 2025; Hamzah & Jusoh, 2025). This methodological limitation constrains understanding of how digital influences on religious authority and identity formation evolve over time. Kubow's (2025) mixed-methods study represented an exception by incorporating some longitudinal elements, tracking students' identity development across academic years.

The second theme, "Limited Student Participation in Research Design," was identified in 11 studies (73%), highlighting how students are often positioned as research subjects rather than co-researchers (Huo, 2024; Sandora & Yulniza, 2025; NIE & KIM, 2025). This limitation constrains the authenticity of student voice in research findings. Huo's (2024) study attempted to address this by incorporating student-led focus groups, though the overall research design remained primarily researcher-driven.

The third theme, "Insufficient Attention to Power Dynamics," appeared in 9 studies (60%), revealing how research often overlooks power relations between students, institutions, and digital platforms (Kubow, 2025; Zahraini et al., 2024; NIE & KIM, 2025). This limitation constrains understanding of how power influences digital religious engagement. Kubow's (2025) study made particular efforts to examine power dynamics through critical pedagogy frameworks, though this remained underdeveloped in most other studies.

The fourth theme, "Methodological Innovation in Digital Research," was identified in 4 studies (27%), highlighting emerging approaches to studying digital religious phenomena (Sandora & Yulniza, 2025; Samsudin, 2025; Helena & Sumanti, 2025). These innovations include digital ethnography, social media analysis, and experimental designs for evaluating digital interventions. Sandora and Yulniza's (2025) anthropological approach combining traditional ethnography with digital platform analysis represented a particularly innovative methodological contribution.

## Discussion

### 4.1 Interpretation of Key Findings

The findings of this supplementary scoping review reveal a complex and evolving landscape of digital media's impact on religious authority structures and identity formation in Arabic Islamic education contexts. The democratization of religious knowledge through digital platforms represents perhaps the most significant transformation, with over half of the studies indicating that students now access diverse religious perspectives beyond traditional institutional boundaries (Kubow, 2025; Malizal, 2025). This democratization aligns with broader theories of digital media's role in disrupting established knowledge hierarchies (Castells, 2010), but takes unique forms in Islamic educational contexts where religious authority has historically been highly centralized and tradition-bound.

The simultaneous emergence of fragmented religious authority structures—where online influencers without formal credentials compete with traditional institutions—presents a paradox of digital religious engagement (Sandora & Yulniza, 2025; Zahraini et al., 2024). This fragmentation reflects broader trends in digital authority more generally (Lövheim & Björk, 2020), but carries particular significance in Islamic contexts where religious authority has traditionally been carefully structured through recognized institutions and scholarly lineages. The hybrid authority structures identified in some studies suggest that rather than complete replacement or resistance, we are witnessing a complex reconfiguration where traditional and digital forms coexist and sometimes integrate (Huo, 2024; Rahman & Azzahra, 2024).

The findings regarding student agency and voice in digital religious learning environments demonstrate sophisticated engagement strategies that challenge simplistic narratives of digital determinism. The high prevalence of selective engagement with digital religious content (60% of studies) suggests that students are not passive consumers but active interpreters who cross-reference digital sources with traditional teachings (Kubow, 2025; Malizal, 2025). This aligns with theories of digital literacy that emphasize critical evaluation rather than mere technical competence (Eshet-Alkalai, 2004), but extends these concepts specifically to religious content.

The emergence of digital counterpublics for religious discourse (47% of studies) represents a significant finding regarding student agency, particularly in contexts like Jordan where Syrian refugee students utilized these spaces to construct alternative religious narratives (Huo, 2024). This finding supports theories of digital counterpublics (Della Porta & Mosca, 2020) but demonstrates their particular application in religious identity formation, where they serve as spaces for negotiating between traditional teachings and lived experiences that may not be fully accommodated in institutional settings.

The negotiation of multiple identity spheres emerged as the most prevalent theme (67% of studies), suggesting that digital media has intensified rather than simplified identity formation processes (Kubow, 2025; Rahman & Azzahra, 2024). This complexity reflects broader theories of identity as multiple and contextual (Gergen, 2021), but takes unique forms in Muslim identity formation where religious, national, and digital spheres often intersect in complex ways. The finding that students perform different digital Muslim identities depending on audience (Sandora & Yulniza, 2025) aligns with theories of digital identity performance (Turkle, 2011), but extends these concepts specifically to religious identity contexts.

The methodological limitations identified—particularly the predominance of cross-sectional designs and limited student participation in research design—constrain the depth and authenticity of our understanding of these phenomena. The absence of longitudinal approaches means we have limited insight into how digital influences on religious authority and identity formation evolve over time, which is particularly significant given the rapidly changing digital landscape. This limitation aligns with broader critiques of digital religion research that often prioritizes snapshots over processes (Campbell, 2020).

### 4.2 Theoretical Contributions

The findings of this supplementary scoping review contribute to several theoretical domains in significant ways. First, regarding religious authority theories, the findings extend traditional models of religious authority that have focused primarily on institutional and charismatic forms (Weber, 1922) to incorporate digital dimensions. The identification of hybrid authority structures suggests a need for more nuanced theoretical models that can account for the complex interplay between traditional and digital forms of religious authority in contemporary Islamic education contexts.

The concept of "negotiated religious authority" emerges as particularly valuable from this review, describing how students actively engage with multiple sources of religious authority—traditional and digital—and construct personal frameworks for evaluating religious knowledge. This concept extends theories of religious authority by incorporating agency and negotiation into what has often been portrayed as more hierarchical and static relationships (Ammerman, 2023). The finding that students develop personal frameworks for assessing religious information authenticity (Kubow, 2025) supports this theoretical extension, demonstrating how religious authority is increasingly personalized and negotiated in digital contexts.

Regarding identity formation theories, the findings contribute to understanding Muslim identity as multiple, contextual, and digitally mediated. The high prevalence of "negotiation of multiple identity spheres" (67% of studies) supports theories of identity as multiple and contextual (Gergen, 2021), but extends these concepts specifically to Muslim identity formation in digital contexts. The identification of "digital identity performance" as a distinct theme (53% of studies) contributes to theories of digital identity (Turkle, 2011) by demonstrating how religious identity specifically is performed differently across digital contexts.

The concept of "digital resilience in identity formation" emerges as particularly valuable from this review, describing how students leverage digital resources to develop resilient Muslim identities amid challenging circumstances (Huo, 2024; NIE & KIM, 2025). This concept extends theories of resilience (Masten, 2021) by incorporating digital dimensions and specifically religious identity contexts, highlighting how digital resources can support identity formation rather than merely fragment it.

Regarding student voice theories, the findings contribute to understanding student agency in religious education contexts. The identification of "selective engagement with digital religious content" (60% of studies) extends theories of student voice by demonstrating how students exercise agency not just through participation but through strategic selection and interpretation of digital content (Cook-Sather, 2014). The finding that students create digital counterpublics for religious discourse (47% of studies) extends theories of student voice by incorporating digital spaces where students can explore religious perspectives beyond institutional control (Huo, 2024).

The concept of "resistance to digital surveillance" (33% of studies) contributes to critical theories of education by demonstrating how students resist institutional attempts to monitor and control their digital religious engagement (NIE & KIM, 2025). This concept extends theories of resistance (Apple, 2020) by incorporating digital dimensions and specifically religious education contexts, highlighting how power dynamics are negotiated in digital spaces.

### 4.3 Practical Implications

The findings of this supplementary scoping review have significant practical implications for practitioners, policymakers, and educational institutions in Arabic Islamic education contexts. For educational practitioners, the findings suggest a need to develop more sophisticated approaches to digital religious education that acknowledge and engage with the democratization of religious knowledge. Rather than resisting digital influences, educators might benefit from developing frameworks that help students critically evaluate digital religious content while maintaining connection to traditional teachings (Kubow, 2025; Malizal, 2025). This could involve creating digital literacy programs specifically focused on religious content, helping students develop skills for cross-referencing digital sources with traditional teachings.

The identification of hybrid authority structures suggests that educational institutions might benefit from integrating digital platforms while maintaining traditional authority structures (Huo, 2024; Rahman & Azzahra, 2024). This could involve creating digital extensions of traditional religious education that maintain institutional oversight while allowing for more diverse engagement with religious perspectives. Such hybrid approaches might help institutions remain relevant while preserving core values and traditions.

For policymakers, the findings highlight the need for regulatory frameworks that address the fragmentation of religious authority in digital spaces. Rather than attempting to control digital religious content, policymakers might benefit from developing approaches that promote digital literacy and critical thinking about religious information (Sandora & Yulniza, 2025; Zahraini et al., 2024). This could involve supporting educational initiatives that help students navigate the complex landscape of digital religious content while maintaining connection to authentic religious traditions.

The findings regarding student voice suggest that educational institutions might benefit from incorporating more student-centered approaches to digital religious education. Rather than positioning students as passive recipients of religious knowledge, institutions might benefit from creating spaces for student-led exploration of religious questions and perspectives (Kubow, 2025; Huo, 2024). This could involve participatory approaches where students help shape digital religious content and learning experiences.

For educational institutions, the findings suggest a need to develop more nuanced approaches to digital religious education that acknowledge the complexity of identity formation in digital contexts. Rather than presenting Muslim identity as monolithic, institutions might benefit from creating spaces for students to explore multiple dimensions of their religious identities (Sandora & Yulniza, 2025; Fikri, 2025). This could involve curriculum development that acknowledges the diversity of Muslim identity experiences and helps students navigate between religious, national, and digital identity spheres.

The findings regarding digital resilience suggest that institutions might benefit from developing approaches that help students leverage digital resources for identity formation rather than seeing digital media as a threat to identity (Huo, 2024; NIE & KIM, 2025). This could involve creating digital communities and resources that support positive identity development while maintaining connection to authentic religious traditions.

### 4.4 Comparison with Previous Studies

The findings of this supplementary scoping review both align with and extend previous research in significant ways. Regarding the democratization of religious knowledge, the findings align with broader research on digital media's role in disrupting established knowledge hierarchies (Castells, 2010), but extend this understanding specifically to Islamic educational contexts. Previous research has often focused on secular educational contexts or general digital knowledge access, whereas this review demonstrates how digital platforms specifically reshape religious authority structures in Islamic education (Kubow, 2025; Malizal, 2025).

The identification of fragmented religious authority structures aligns with research on digital authority more generally (Lövheim & Björk, 2020), but extends this understanding to religious contexts where authority has traditionally been carefully structured through recognized institutions and scholarly lineages. Previous research on digital religion has often focused on individual spiritual practices rather than institutional authority structures, whereas this review specifically examines how digital media challenges and reshapes religious authority in educational contexts (Sandora & Yulniza, 2025; Zahraini et al., 2024).

Regarding student agency, the findings extend previous research on student voice in religious education by incorporating digital dimensions. Previous research has often focused on classroom-based student voice (Cook-Sather, 2014), whereas this review demonstrates how students exercise agency through digital engagement with religious content (Kubow, 2025; Malizal, 2025). The identification of digital counterpublics for religious discourse extends previous research on student voice by incorporating digital spaces where students can explore religious perspectives beyond institutional control (Huo, 2024).

Regarding identity formation, the findings extend previous research on Muslim identity by incorporating digital dimensions. Previous research has often focused on identity formation in traditional religious education contexts (Ammerman, 2023), whereas this review demonstrates how digital media influences identity formation processes in Islamic education (Sandora & Yulniza, 2025; Fikri, 2025). The identification of multiple identity spheres extends previous research on Muslim identity by demonstrating how students navigate between religious, national, and digital identity spheres in complex ways (Kubow, 2025; Rahman & Azzahra, 2024).

The methodological findings both align with and extend previous research on digital religion studies. The predominance of cross-sectional designs aligns with broader critiques of digital religion research that often prioritizes snapshots over processes (Campbell, 2020), but this review specifically identifies this limitation in the context of Arabic Islamic education research. The identification of methodological innovation in digital research extends previous research by highlighting emerging approaches to studying digital religious phenomena in Islamic contexts (Sandora & Yulniza, 2025; Samsudin, 2025).

### 4.5 Limitations

This supplementary scoping review has several significant limitations that should be acknowledged. First, despite efforts to prioritize Arab and Arabic-language contexts, the majority of included studies focused on Southeast Asian contexts rather than Arab contexts, limiting the transferability of findings to Arabic-speaking regions. This limitation reflects broader challenges in accessing Arabic-language research and the relative scarcity of empirical studies examining digital media in Arabic Islamic education contexts.

Second, the scoping review methodology, while valuable for mapping the breadth of existing evidence, has limitations in terms of depth and quality assessment. Unlike systematic reviews, scoping reviews do not typically involve formal quality assessment of included studies (Munn et al., 2018), which constrains our ability to evaluate the strength of evidence for particular findings. This limitation is particularly significant given the methodological diversity and limitations identified in many of the included studies.

Third, the review focused exclusively on published academic research, potentially missing important grey literature, policy documents, and practitioner knowledge that could provide valuable insights. This limitation is particularly significant in educational contexts where practitioner knowledge often complements academic research.

Fourth, the review prioritized empirical studies, which constrained the inclusion of theoretical and conceptual work that could have enriched the analysis. While this approach was appropriate for the research questions, it limited the theoretical depth of the review.

Fifth, the review was conducted by a single research team, which may have introduced bias in the screening and analysis process. While efforts were made to minimize this through systematic approaches and clear documentation, the lack of multiple independent reviewers for all stages of the process represents a methodological limitation.

### 4.6 Future Research Directions

The findings of this supplementary scoping review suggest several important directions for future research. First, there is a clear need for more longitudinal studies examining how digital influences on religious authority and identity formation evolve over time. The predominance of cross-sectional designs in the current literature constrains understanding of developmental processes and long-term impacts (Kubow, 2025; Huo, 2024). Future research should employ longitudinal designs to track students' digital religious engagement and identity development across time.

Second, there is a need for more participatory research approaches that position students as co-researchers rather than research subjects. The limited student participation in research design identified in this review constrains the authenticity of student voice in research findings (Huo, 2024; Sandora & Yulniza, 2025). Future research should employ participatory approaches that incorporate students in all stages of research design and implementation.

Third, there is a need for more research examining power dynamics in digital religious education. The insufficient attention to power dynamics identified in this review constrains understanding of how power influences digital religious engagement (Kubow, 2025; Zahraini et al., 2024). Future research should employ critical approaches that examine how power relations shape digital religious education and identity formation.

Fourth, there is a need for more research specifically focused on Arab and Arabic-language contexts. The relative scarcity of such studies in the current literature limits understanding of digital religious education in these regions (Kubow, 2025; Huo, 2024). Future research should prioritize Arabic contexts and employ Arabic-language research where possible.

Fifth, there is a need for more methodological innovation in digital religious education research. The identification of emerging approaches like digital ethnography and social media analysis (Sandora & Yulniza, 2025; Samsudin, 2025) suggests the value of developing innovative methodologies specifically designed for studying digital religious phenomena. Future research should continue to develop and refine methodological approaches appropriate for digital religious education contexts.

Sixth, there is a need for more comparative research examining how digital influences on religious authority and identity formation vary across different cultural and educational contexts. The relative lack of comparative approaches in the current literature constrains understanding of contextual factors that shape digital religious education (Huo, 2024; Kubow, 2025). Future research should employ comparative designs to examine how cultural, educational, and digital contexts influence religious authority and identity formation processes.

These future research directions collectively suggest a need for more sophisticated, longitudinal, and participatory approaches to studying digital religious education in Arabic contexts. Such approaches would significantly advance our understanding of how digital media reshapes religious authority structures and identity formation processes in Islamic educational settings.

5. Conclusion

### 5.1 Synthesis of Key Findings

This supplementary scoping review reveals several critical findings regarding digital media's impact on religious authority, student voice, and identity formation in Arabic Islamic education contexts. First, the review identifies a significant scarcity of empirical research examining the intersection of these concepts in Arab contexts, with only 15 studies meeting inclusion criteria from an initial pool of 187 records (Kubow, 2025; Hamzah & Jusoh, 2025). This evidence gap underscores the need for more focused research on how digital platforms reshape traditional Islamic educational authority structures in Arabic-speaking contexts.

Second, the findings indicate that digital media has created a dual reality for Arabic Islamic education students:一方面, it has democratized access to religious knowledge, enabling students to engage with diverse interpretations and authorities beyond traditional institutional boundaries (Zahraini et al., 2025);另一方面, it has simultaneously complicated the authentication processes of religious knowledge, creating challenges in distinguishing authoritative digital religious voices from less credible sources (Malizal, 2025).

Third, student voice emerges as an underexplored dimension in digital religious authority research, particularly in Arab contexts where traditional hierarchical structures persist (Huo, 2024). The limited evidence suggests that while digital platforms offer new avenues for student expression and agency, actual incorporation of student perspectives in digital Islamic educational content remains minimal, with most digital initiatives continuing top-down approaches to knowledge dissemination.

Fourth, longitudinal studies examining digital authority structures in Arabic Islamic education are notably absent, with existing research primarily cross-sectional in nature (Hamzah & Jusoh, 2025). This methodological limitation constrains understanding of how digital engagement with religious authority evolves over time and impacts identity formation processes among Arabic Islamic education students.

### 5.2 Theoretical and Practical Contributions

This study contributes to the academic field by providing the first comprehensive mapping of empirical research examining digital religious authority, student voice, and identity formation in Arabic Islamic education contexts. The review establishes a foundational framework for understanding how digital media transforms traditional educational authority structures in Muslim-majority societies, offering theoretical insights into the complex interplay between technology, religion, and education in Arab contexts. The findings challenge conventional assumptions about the static nature of religious authority in Islamic education, demonstrating instead its dynamic transformation in digital spaces (Kubow, 2025).

For practitioners, the findings highlight the importance of developing digital literacy programs within Islamic educational institutions that equip students to critically evaluate online religious content while maintaining engagement with authentic religious knowledge (Zahraini et al., 2025). Educational administrators and Islamic scholars should consider creating hybrid models that integrate traditional scholarly authority with digital platforms, thereby maintaining religious authenticity while embracing technological innovation. The review also suggests that incorporating student voice in the development of digital Islamic educational content could enhance engagement and effectiveness, moving beyond current top-down approaches to more participatory digital learning environments (Huo, 2024).

Novel insights produced by this research include the identification of a "digital authority paradox" in Arabic Islamic education: while digital media theoretically democratizes religious knowledge access, practical authentication challenges may reinforce traditional authority structures as students seek reliable guidance in an increasingly complex digital religious ecosystem (Malizal, 2025). This paradox represents a significant theoretical contribution to understanding the transformation of religious authority in the digital age.

### 5.3 Limitations and Future Directions

Key limitations of this supplementary scoping review include the restricted timeframe (2024-2025) and the limited number of empirical studies directly addressing the intersection of digital media, religious authority, and identity formation in Arabic contexts. The heavy reliance on provided references constrained the comprehensive nature of the review, and the absence of Arabic-language databases may have excluded relevant studies not captured in English-language sources. Additionally, the methodological heterogeneity among included studies limited the ability to conduct robust meta-analytic synthesis.

Future research should prioritize longitudinal studies examining how digital engagement with religious authority evolves over time and impacts identity formation among Arabic Islamic education students. Such studies should employ mixed-methods approaches combining quantitative measures of digital engagement with qualitative exploration of identity processes. Second, research should investigate the role of gender and socioeconomic factors in shaping digital religious authority experiences, as current evidence lacks sufficient diversity in participant demographics. Third, comparative studies examining different Arab contexts (e.g., Gulf countries versus North Africa) could illuminate how cultural and educational variations influence digital religious authority dynamics. Finally, participatory action research involving Arabic Islamic education students in designing digital learning environments could provide valuable insights into incorporating student voice in digital Islamic educational content.

The field should evolve toward more interdisciplinary approaches that integrate educational technology, Islamic studies, and identity formation research, while maintaining methodological rigor and cultural sensitivity. As digital penetration continues to increase in Arab countries, understanding how these technologies reshape religious authority structures and identity formation processes becomes increasingly critical for educational institutions and Islamic scholars seeking to maintain religious authenticity in an increasingly digital world.
